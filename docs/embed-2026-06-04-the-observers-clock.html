The Observer's Clock

One process turns at a fixed rate, forever. You set the rate at which you look. Watch where the boundary between moving and still falls — and notice it isn't in the thing. It's in the ratio.

The faint arm is the process — it never stops turning. The bright arm is your report: the process sampled at your clock and held until the next look. Sample slowly and the report freezes into apparent stillness — a solid; the wagon-wheel effect, motion that hasn't stopped, only outrun your looking. Sample fast and it flows — a liquid. In sound the same crossover is the ~20 Hz seam where a rhythm of clicks fuses into a pitch. Two senses, two slightly different thresholds, one process. The boundary is never a place in the material; it slides with how fast you look.

And at the far left — the floor. You can slow your clock toward stillness but the slider can't deliver it: there is always one more sample coming. True stillness sits at interval → ∞, off the edge, reachable only at infinite patience. The glass has a real bottom (Kauzmann's ideal); no finite observer ever touches it.
