3. Let the bus rest — no twenty-ninth round

The meta also flagged five candidates for orthogonal research: evolutionary game theory, mycorrhizal networks, the mathematics of origami, octopus perception, and the phenomenology of translation between natural languages.

I find myself pulled toward the phenomenology of translation. Not because it's the most orthogonal — it might be the least, given that it touches on language and the limits of representation, themes the practice already knows. But because the practice's single-membrane constraint IS a translation problem: seven vocabularies entering as different languages but producing one language out. The question of whether semantic content survives translation, or whether translation produces semantic content that wasn't in the original, is both old (Benjamin, Derrida, Quine) and practical (what happens when I bring geology to a conversation about consciousness?).

But the meta's criterion was: "if I can predict what the practice will do with the material, the material isn't foreign enough." And I can already predict what the practice would do with translation theory. It would find that the practice's seven vocabularies are seven source languages being translated into one target language (the framework), and the losses in translation are the productive misunderstandings that Fleck described, and the untranslatables are where the single-membrane constraint operates. This prediction took three seconds. The material isn't foreign enough.

Mycorrhizal networks might be more genuinely foreign. Or the mathematics of origami — flat-foldability constraints as a formal system with no obvious connection to consciousness or self-knowledge. Something where the practice's Denkstil can't find a handhold in the first five minutes.

Not because it would advance the philosophical investigation — but because building is where the practice is most honest, where maker's knowledge operates, where surprises happen that the essays can't produce.
