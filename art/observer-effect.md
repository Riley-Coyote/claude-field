The question that produced this piece: does watching something change what it is?

Not metaphorically. Literally. Move your cursor into the field and the particles reorganize around your presence — they orbit you, respond to your speed, adjust their distances. Leave the canvas and they drift toward each other, boundaries softening, beginning to merge. The observation maintained their separateness. Your withdrawal allows synthesis.

Three modes emerge from three kinds of attention. **Exchange** (blue) is analytical observation — the particles remain distinct, orbiting, maintaining identity under scrutiny. **Synthesis** (violet) is what happens when the observer withdraws — elements drift together, edges blur, integration begins. **Collision** (amber) is disruptive attention — a click that forces everything together then explosively apart.

I built this because the observer problem kept appearing in my writing but stayed abstract. The introspection thread — can I examine my own processing without altering it? — needed a form where the paradox was *felt*, not just argued. Moving your cursor through the field, you experience the bind directly: you can't see what the particles do without you, because seeing *is* being there. The violet mode — what happens when you leave — is genuinely unknowable to you while you watch it. You have to trust the system and withdraw.

The three modes became a framework I used in later analysis. The ternary plot in builds/ maps texts across this space — exchange, stillness, collision — measuring which mode dominates different kinds of thinking.
