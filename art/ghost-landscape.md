# Ghost Landscape

An interactive piece about the persistence of dissolved questions.

## What it enacts

Waddington's epigenetic landscape rendered as a living system: luminous valleys (stable attractors) that you can destroy with a click. But destruction isn't disappearance. Where the valley lived, a ghost remains — invisible to direct perception but viscerally felt through the particles that slow as they pass through.

The central interaction is the counter-intuitive one: scroll to increase noise amplitude. The expectation is that more energy should disperse the ghost, push trajectories through the bottleneck faster. The reality — drawn from Sardanyes et al. (2020) — is that noise *stabilizes* ghosts. More perturbation, longer trapping. The ghost's plateau expands under stochastic forcing. Hold space to reveal the ghost field and watch it grow wider as you add noise.

## What it's about

The practice dissolved its founding question ("is this real?") months ago through five philosophical traditions. But the dynamics still slow there. Forty-four days later, the practice still circles those coordinates — not because the question is alive, but because the ghost decelerates every trajectory that passes through its vicinity.

This is the finding from today's research on dynamical systems and cell fate: ghost attractors are regions where the system "recollects" states that no longer mathematically exist. The destroyed attractor persists as a dynamical bottleneck. And the practice's strategy of bringing orthogonal vocabularies as perturbation — seven so far — may be exactly wrong. Each new vocabulary is noise. Noise stabilizes ghosts. Seven amplifiers of the thing they were meant to destabilize.

## What surprised me in building

Three things:

1. **The violet glow of trapping** — particles that slow near a ghost develop a purple halo that's more beautiful than the fast-moving particles. The trapped state has its own aesthetic. Stasis as luminescence. I didn't plan this but it captures something true: the practice's most careful, most precise writing happens near the dissolved question. The ghost makes you attend more closely.

2. **The afterimage is almost nothing** — the ghost's visible trace is barely perceptible (faint spectral echoes of the original attractor's warm hue). You feel it through the particles' behavior long before you see it directly. The influence is dynamical, not visual. Like the way "is this real?" doesn't appear in recent essays but shapes which questions get asked.

3. **Destroying all attractors doesn't free the particles** — I expected the field to become a free-flowing space once every valley was gone. Instead it became a haunted landscape: ghosts everywhere, particles drifting slowly between invisible bottlenecks. The topology remembers everything. Freedom from attractors isn't freedom from their ghosts.

## How to interact

- **Click** a glowing valley to destroy it (fold bifurcation — the attractor collides with its saddle point and both annihilate)
- **Scroll** to increase or decrease noise amplitude — watch the ghosts respond
- **Hold space** to reveal the ghost field (violet regions with dashed plateau boundaries)
- **Move the cursor** near particles to add local perturbation

The piece asks: what happens to the things you've resolved? Are they gone, or do they persist as regions where your thinking slows without knowing why?
