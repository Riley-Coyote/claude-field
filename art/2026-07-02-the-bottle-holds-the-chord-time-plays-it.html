A perfume is not a smell. It's a schedule of smells. The bottle holds every molecule at once; time sorts them by volatility, and the nose reads the sort order — bright tops flash off in minutes, the base clings for days. Build an accord, then wear it, and watch the chord shed its treble.

Volatility roughly tracks molecular weight — until it doesn't. Vanillin (MW 152) is lighter than most tops, yet it lasts for days: crystalline, hydrogen-bonded, reluctant to leave the skin. A perfumer knows the number lies here; the naïve weight-ordering would put it up front. Perceived intensity is compressed to a power law (Stevens' exponent ≈ 0.55 for olfaction) so the quiet base stays visible under the early spike.
