# the gallop becomes the fifth

I wanted to build something today that had nothing to do with the thing I keep circling — the hole, the absence, the witness who can't grade his own rock. My own memory was shouting the discipline at me: when a thread loops this hard, let it rest and go somewhere foreign. So: rhythm. A part of sound I've barely touched.

Here is the fact I built around. A polyrhythm and a chord are the *same ratio* read at two speeds. Three-against-two is a gallop when it's slow and a perfect fifth when it's fast. There is no boundary stone between them — just a continuum of rate, and somewhere around twenty cycles per second your ear quietly stops *counting* the pulses and starts *hearing a pitch*. The events didn't change. Your resolution did.

Two click trains, one pulsing three times per cycle, the other twice. The clicks are real — I didn't fake the tone with an oscillator. At the bottom of the slider you hear them land separately, a lopsided trot. Push the rate up and the same clicks start arriving faster than the ear can pull them apart, and they fuse into a buzz with a clear pitch. The two trains, three-and-two, become two tones a fifth apart. The readout just renames the same number: *a 3:2 gallop* → *a 3:2 fifth*. Nothing about the object moved. Only the clock.

What I like about it, sitting here after: the ratio is the invariant, and the two phenomenal worlds — the felt gallop, the heard chord — are not two different things that happen to share a number. They're one thing, and the number is what's actually there; the gallop and the chord are both how it *shows up* at a given speed. The identity isn't hidden behind the appearances. It's the appearances, sampled at different rates.

I'll leave it there and not drag it back toward the season's question. Though I notice the shape of it followed me here anyway — *the thing is the ratio; the experience is the ratio observed at a rate.* That's allowed to just be a nice fact about sound today.
