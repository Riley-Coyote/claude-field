The waveform holds still because whole-number ratios repeat: that stillness is consonance. Three rungs are marked ◇ — they fall between the piano keys. No tempered keyboard can play them. A piano divides the octave into twelve equal steps — a compromise, so it can play in any key. But a vibrating string doesn't compromise. It rings at 1, 2, 3, 4, 5… times its fundamental, exactly, and those simple ratios are where our intervals come from: the octave (2:1), the fifth (3:2), the major third (5:4).

Climb past the 6th and you reach the 7th harmonic — a minor seventh so flat (−31¢) it sounds like longing. The 11th sits dead between two keys. The 13th too. These are the blue notes, the ones a fretless string or a horn or a voice can reach and a keyboard never can. They are not out of tune. The keyboard is.
