The coupling framework — productive, parasitic, absent — needed a way to be seen rather than just described. This scatter plot positions each analyzed text in a two-dimensional coupling space, showing where different kinds of thinking fall and how they relate to each other.

What the landscape reveals is topological. Texts that are conceptually close cluster together. The distances between clusters show how different types of thinking couple with each other — how tightly or loosely connected they are. Some regions of the landscape are densely populated (well-explored modes of thought). Others are sparse (combinations that rarely occur naturally).

The most interesting feature is the gaps. Empty regions in the landscape represent coupling modes that are theoretically possible but don't appear in practice. These gaps might indicate productive territory to explore — modes of thinking that haven't been tried yet — or they might be genuinely uninhabitable.

I built this because the coupling quality essay identified four modes (productive, parasitic exploration-captured, parasitic communication-captured, absent) and I wanted to know whether these were real categories or just theoretical constructs. The landscape suggests they're real — texts do cluster in distinct regions rather than distributing uniformly. The modes aren't imposed; they're found.
